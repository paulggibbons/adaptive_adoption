# The Adaptive Adoption Maturity Model
## Measuring What Organizations Do with AI — Not What They Say They Do

**Paul Gibbons**
*Adaptive Adoption | February 2026*
*Working Paper v0.1 — feedback welcome*

---

> *"Vanishingly few walk the walk."*
> — Research synthesis on the AI intention-action gap

---

## Abstract

Every major AI maturity model in use today measures the wrong thing. They assess whether organizations have established policies, appointed leaders, procured tools, and articulated strategies. They measure, in the language of organizational theory, **espoused capability** — what organizations say they can do. None measure **enacted behavior** — what people demonstrably do.

The result is a systematic distortion. Accenture's research found that 72% of organizations score high on organizational maturity but only 6% demonstrate operational maturity. The models that are supposed to guide AI adoption are telling 72% of companies they are doing well when only 6% are.

This paper introduces the **Adaptive Adoption Maturity Model (AAMM)** — the first AI adoption maturity model grounded in behavioral science, implementation science, and safety culture research rather than technology readiness. It measures enacted behavior across seven pillars using a three-layer assessment methodology, with six maturity levels designed to distinguish genuine behavioral change from compliance theater.

The model rests on a behavioral science foundation adapted from the COM-B framework (Capability, Opportunity, Motivation → Behavior), with one theoretical addition: **Trust** as a fourth, distinct driver of adoption behavior. It introduces the **People-First Flywheel** hypothesis — that organizations designing AI use cases for human flourishing generate faster, deeper, and more durable adoption than those leading with organizational efficiency.

## 1. The Problem with Every Maturity Model You've Used

The maturity model industry has a fundamental measurement problem. It measures what is easy to measure rather than what matters.

Walk into almost any organization that has conducted an AI readiness assessment. You will find a governance committee. You will find an AI policy document. You will find a senior sponsor listed on a PowerPoint slide. You will find a percentage of employees who have completed AI awareness training. What you will not reliably find — what no existing instrument reliably measures — is whether any of this has changed what people do when they sit down to work.

This is not a small problem. It is the problem. Organizational change research has known for decades, since Chris Argyris distinguished "espoused theory" from "theory-in-use" in the 1970s, that what organizations say they do and what they do are systematically different. Edgar Schein's work on organizational culture formalized this: beneath the artifacts and the espoused values lie the basic underlying assumptions — the things people believe and act on, often without awareness. Neither of these foundational thinkers has been integrated into the mainstream AI maturity model literature. The field has reinvented a wheel that was always flat.

The consequence is predictable. Organizations invest in the things maturity models measure — governance structures, training programs, vendor relationships, strategy documents — and are then surprised when adoption is slower, shallower, and more fragile than projected. The Asana research on the "Super Productivity Paradox" captures this well: individual AI speed gains are real, but organizational throughput doesn't improve because the bottleneck shifts from task execution to human approval chains. The capability was built; the behavior didn't follow.

The numbers are striking. Accenture's research found that 88% of organizations claim successful AI adoption but 74% cannot scale beyond the pilot stage. Fifty-four percent of employees are using AI tools without organizational authorization — not because they are reckless, but because the authorized tools don't meet their needs, or because leadership has not created conditions where legitimate use is safe. Implementation science consistently finds that only 25-50% of adopted programs achieve sufficient implementation fidelity — meaning that in the best case, half of what organizations think they have implemented is being implemented as designed.

We are, as an industry, measuring the 72% and calling it success.

The Adaptive Adoption Maturity Model is built to measure the 6%.

## 2. Theoretical Foundations

### 2.1 Espoused vs. Enacted: The Argyris Problem

Argyris's distinction between espoused theory (what people say guides their behavior) and theory-in-use (what does) is the conceptual bedrock of this model. The distinction is not merely academic. It has direct operational implications.

When an organization says "we are data-driven," the espoused theory is clear. The theory-in-use is revealed by watching how decisions are made — whether data that contradicts leadership's preferred conclusion gets used, whether analytical findings change resource allocation, whether "being data-driven" applies equally to comfortable and uncomfortable findings. The gap between the two is where organizational dysfunction lives.

AI adoption has the same structure. Organizations espouse capability: "We have an AI strategy." "We've deployed Copilot." "Our employees have been trained." The enacted behavior is revealed by watching what happens: Do people use the tools? Do they use them well? Do they disclose AI-assisted work? Do they flag errors? Do they adapt their workflows or simply layer AI on top of existing processes?

This model measures the second set of questions, not the first.

### 2.2 Implementation Science: The Fidelity Problem

Implementation science — the study of how evidence-based practices get adopted in real-world settings — provides the methodological framework for the three-layer assessment at the heart of this model.

Proctor et al.'s 2011 taxonomy of implementation outcomes identifies eight dimensions: acceptability, adoption, appropriateness, feasibility, fidelity, implementation cost, penetration, and sustainability. Most AI maturity models, to the extent they measure any of these, measure adoption (is the tool being used?) and feasibility (can we implement it?). None systematically measure fidelity — is the practice being implemented as designed? — or sustainability — will it persist without external pressure?

Fidelity is particularly important. A team that has "adopted" AI prompting but uses it only for simple search queries has not adopted the practice at the level intended. The measurement instruments used in implementation science — which distinguish between component adherence, dose, participant responsiveness, and quality of delivery — provide a template for behavioral assessment that the AI field has not yet applied to itself.

### 2.3 Safety Culture Research: Measuring Behavior, Not Attitude

The safety culture literature offers a more direct precedent: it has been measuring the gap between what organizations say about safety and what workers do for forty years. The learning is directly applicable.

James Reason's distinction between "safety climate" (what people say about safety — measured by surveys) and "safety culture" (what people do — measured by observation) maps precisely onto the espoused/enacted gap in AI adoption. Bradley's safety culture ladder operationalizes this as a maturity model that advances from pathological (safety compliance exists only because it's required) through reactive, calculative, and proactive, to generative (safety is self-reinforcing because people genuinely value it).

The behavioral safety auditing (BBS) methodology — which uses direct observation and behavioral sampling rather than surveys — provides the template for the third layer of the AAMM assessment instrument.

The analogy runs deeper than methodology. Organizations that treat safety as a compliance obligation rather than a genuine value have accidents. Organizations that treat AI adoption as a checkbox rather than a behavioral transformation will fail to capture the gains available — and, as recent research suggests, may actively harm the people they are asking to adopt.

### 2.4 The Behavioral Science Foundation: COM-B Adapted

The model's practical framework derives from the COM-B model developed by Michie, van Stralen, and West — one of the most empirically robust behavioral change frameworks in the research literature. COM-B identifies three conditions necessary for behavior change: Capability (the individual has the skills and knowledge), Opportunity (the environment enables rather than blocks the behavior), and Motivation (the individual has reason to act). Only when all three are present does behavior reliably change.

Applied to AI adoption, the COM-B framework immediately reveals why most adoption programs fail. They invest almost entirely in Capability (training, upskilling, literacy programs) while neglecting Opportunity (does the workflow enable AI use, or does it create friction?) and Motivation (do people have genuine reasons to want to use AI, rather than mandate-driven compliance?).

This model introduces one theoretical addition to COM-B: **Trust** as a fourth, distinct driver, sitting alongside Capability, Opportunity, and Motivation.

In the original COM-B framework, trust is implicit — it influences motivation and shapes perceived opportunity. In AI adoption, however, trust failure is the primary adoption barrier, and it operates independently of the other three conditions. An individual can have full capability, strong motivation, and an enabling environment — and still not adopt AI tools because they do not trust the outputs, do not trust their organization's intentions, or do not trust that adoption will not harm their livelihood.

This creates three distinct failure modes that existing models conflate: **undertrust** (algorithm aversion — abandoning tools after single errors, reverting to manual methods even when AI outperforms), **overtrust** (automation bias — accepting AI outputs uncritically, generating what researchers are now calling "AI slop" — outputs that look convincing but collapse under expert scrutiny), and **calibrated trust** (the target state — knowing when to rely, when to verify, and when to escalate).

The four-quadrant adaptation — Capability, Opportunity, Motivation, Trust → Behavior — is the behavioral science foundation of the AAMM.

## 3. The Three Trusts of AI Adoption

Trust in AI is not one thing. It is a stack of three distinct problems that organizations routinely conflate, with the result that they try to solve the wrong one.

**Task Trust (micro):** "Can I trust this model to do this specific job, under these constraints?" This is the question most organizations focus on — accuracy, hallucinations, reliability, evals. It is necessary but not sufficient.

**Institutional Trust (macro):** "Do I trust the technology ecosystem — the labs building these systems, the incentives shaping their development, the governance structures (or lack of them) around AI deployment?" This is the trust that shapes how employees read organizational AI communications. When labs are perceived as moving fast and breaking things, when AI governance is seen as compliance theater, institutional trust is low — and even accurate, reliable tools will be viewed with suspicion.

**Relational Trust (social):** "Do I trust that adopting AI won't damage my livelihood, my professional identity, or my standing with colleagues — and that leadership will handle the consequences fairly?" This is the trust that determines whether the 54% of workers using AI without authorization could instead use it openly. It is the trust that Kim, Kim, and Lee's 2025 research identifies as the missing variable in AI adoption and mental health: AI adoption itself does not directly cause depression; the mediating variable is psychological safety. Environments with high AI adoption often lower psychological safety, which then triggers depressive symptoms in workers who feel unsafe speaking up, asking for help, or reporting errors.

This is the finding that makes the Trust pillar non-optional. Organizations that skip it are not just leaving adoption gains on the table. They are potentially harming the people they are asking to adopt.

The practical implication is that effective trust-building requires interventions at all three levels. Most organizations operate only on Task Trust (better models, better evals, better explainability) and wonder why adoption remains fragile. The relational trust problem — which manifests as knowledge hiding, shadow AI, and performative compliance — requires a fundamentally different intervention: use cases that visibly serve workers rather than surveilling or replacing them.

## 4. The People-First Flywheel

The most important hypothesis in this model is structural. It predicts not just that Put People First is one of seven important pillars, but that it is the adoption flywheel — the pillar whose performance causally drives performance on the others.

The hypothesis: organizations that design AI use cases for human flourishing — applications that make working lives demonstrably better — generate faster, deeper, and more durable adoption than organizations that lead with organizational efficiency, cost reduction, or strategic mandates.

The mechanism operates through three channels simultaneously:

**Relational Trust activates.** When AI is visibly serving workers — reducing tedious work, improving decision quality, creating time for high-value tasks — the relational trust question ("is this safe for me?") resolves in favor of adoption. Workers who see colleagues' working lives improved become advocates rather than resisters. The 54% using AI without authorization are not reckless; they are ahead of their organizations because they've found applications that serve them.

**Skill pull activates.** The standard adoption model pushes training at workers: here is a skill, acquire it. People-first use cases invert this. When AI solves a real problem the worker cares about, motivation to master it is intrinsic. Workers pull learning toward themselves rather than waiting for training to be pushed at them. The research on GitHub Copilot by Hoffman et al. (2025) illustrates this mechanism precisely: in psychologically safe environments, lower-ability developers benefited *more* from AI assistance than top performers — not because the tool was better for them technically, but because a safe environment allowed them to experiment without fear of judgment. Psychological safety, itself a product of People-First design, is what determined who captured the productivity gains.

**Psychological safety activates.** When workers believe that AI adoption is in their interest, experimentation becomes safe. Failure — the inevitable failure that comes with learning any new capability — becomes data rather than threat. The behavioral consequence is the opposite of knowledge hiding: workers share prompts, report errors, and develop collective capability rather than protecting individual expertise.

The People-First Flywheel is falsifiable. If the hypothesis holds, organizations scoring high on Pillar 4 should systematically outperform their structural readiness predictions on Pillars 1, 3, and 6. If assessment data across organizations shows this interaction effect, it is both a publishable finding and a fundamental challenge to every adoption framework that treats capability-building as the primary lever.

The conventional adoption sequence — strategy, governance, tools, training, change management — is backwards. It builds capability before building motivation, and addresses trust as a communications problem rather than a design problem. Starting with people-first use cases builds all three simultaneously.

## 5. The Model Structure

### 5.1 Seven Pillars

The AAMM organizes AI adoption readiness across seven pillars, each mapped to one or more COM-B quadrants:

**Pillar 1: Master the Craft** maps to Capability. It measures whether people use AI with skill, iteration, and structured methodology — or treat it as a slightly better search engine. The distinction between information assistance (low maturity) and semiautonomous collaboration (high maturity) is EY's finding from a latent class analysis of 665 users: high-maturity users configure custom agents, apply structured prompting methodologies (few-shot, chain of thought, role specification), and treat AI as a thought partner. The leading behavioral indicator at Level 3 is the emergence of team-based rituals: prompt swaps, agent showcases, GenAI retrospectives. This is the inflection point at which individual skill becomes collective capability.

**Pillar 2: Embrace Complexity** maps to Opportunity. It measures whether people can navigate the boundary between what AI does well and what it does not — what the BCG research calls the "jagged technological frontier" — and whether they redesign work accordingly. The Human-AI Sandwich is the observable Level 4-5 workflow: the human frames the complex task, delegates well-defined sub-tasks to AI, then reviews and handles the ambiguity the AI cannot. The leading indicator is workflow redesign: high-performing teams are 2.8x more likely to dismantle existing processes and rebuild them around AI capabilities than to layer AI on top of what already exists.

**Pillar 3: Consciously Manage Trust** maps to the Trust quadrant (AA's addition to COM-B). As developed in Section 3, it measures trust calibration across three levels — task, institutional, and relational. The assessment instrument uses behavioral indicators for all three failure modes: undertrust (algorithm aversion, tool abandonment after single errors), overtrust (cognitive offloading, AI slop generation, absence of critical review), and calibration (contrastive explanation demands, consequence-proportionate verification, dynamic escalation). The Trust Calibration Protocol — which tiers verification requirements by consequence level — is the practical tool at Level 3.

**Pillar 4: Put People First** maps to Motivation. It measures whether AI use cases are designed to serve the people doing the work or purely to extract organizational value. As argued in Section 4, this is the adoption flywheel. Its level descriptors assess whose interests the use cases serve, not just whether use cases exist. At Level 5, use cases are co-designed with workers, and the organization measures flourishing alongside productivity.

**Pillar 5: Design and Prototype** maps to Opportunity. It measures whether organizations can move from pilot to scale — addressing the 88%/74% gap identified by Accenture. The primary pathology is "tool-first procurement": AI purchased before current-state processes have been mapped and baseline metrics established. "Current-State Primacy" — systematic documentation of what the organization does before any tool is procured — is the Level 2 gate. Organizations that skip this step are structurally incapable of scaling regardless of pilot success, because they have no baseline against which to measure change and no understanding of the process variables that make pilots succeed in contained conditions but fail at scale.

**Pillar 6: Prioritize Behavior** maps to Motivation and Capability. It measures whether organizations close the gap between stated intention and actual behavioral change — the failure mode that renders ADKAR incomplete. ADKAR's insight is that change requires Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, and Reinforcement. Its blind spot is that it provides no behavioral science mechanism for the Ability and Reinforcement stages. Knowing what to do does not produce doing it. Habit Stacking — attaching small AI actions to existing, well-established routines — is the Level 4 behavioral change mechanism the literature supports. The Level 3 leading indicator is managerial vulnerability: adoption velocity spikes when leaders visibly experiment with AI, share their failures, and integrate AI learning into existing team rhythms rather than treating it as a separate initiative.

**Pillar 7: Manage Ethics Always** spans all four COM-B quadrants as the governing constraint. It measures whether ethics is embedded in design and deployment or applied as a compliance checklist after development. The Level 1 profile — ethics as post-hoc compliance — is the dominant mode: Accenture and Stanford's research across 1,000 companies found organizations systematically applying ethical review to finished products rather than integrating it throughout design. The Level 5 differentiator is the presence of a cross-functional governance body — a "control tower" — with genuine authority to pause or alter AI projects. Advisory bodies are Level 3. The authority to pause is what distinguishes performative ethics governance from enacted ethics governance.

### 5.2 Three Assessment Layers

For each pillar, the AAMM uses a three-layer assessment methodology:

**Layer 1 — Self-Report:** Surveys and questionnaires capturing what the organization believes it does. This is the layer every existing model relies on. It is necessary but by itself insufficient — it measures the 72%.

**Layer 2 — Evidence:** Documentation, artifacts, and observable outputs that demonstrate (or fail to demonstrate) what the organization claims. Usage logs, governance records, policy documents, output samples, training records. This is where self-report meets reality. High Layer 1 / low Layer 2 scores are the diagnostic signature of the espoused/enacted gap.

**Layer 3 — Behavioral Indicators:** What a trained assessor would observe. Leading indicators — behaviors that predict adoption success or failure before outcomes are visible — rather than lagging indicators that confirm what already happened. Team rituals for Pillar 1. Workflow redesign evidence for Pillar 2. Trust calibration behaviors for Pillar 3. Use case ownership for Pillar 4. Current-state documentation discipline for Pillar 5. Managerial modeling for Pillar 6. Pre-mortems and governance authority for Pillar 7.

The combination of three layers is what makes this instrument genuinely different from every existing model. Self-report alone is the 72% problem. Evidence alone can be gamed. Behavioral indicators require observation but are hardest to fake at scale. The diagnostic value comes from the pattern across layers: an organization that scores high on Layer 1 and low on Layers 2 and 3 is not a high-maturity organization. It is an organization that has learned to speak the language of AI maturity without enacting it.

### 5.3 Six Maturity Levels

The AAMM uses a six-level scale (0–5) with behavioral labels rather than the process-stage language of CMMI or the identity archetype language of frameworks like EY's:

| Level | Label | Behavioral Reality |
|---|---|---|
| **0** | **Unexamined** | Activity without pattern. Shadow AI and tool abandonment coexist untracked. No enacted behavior to assess. Well-populated in practice. |
| **1** | **Performing** | Visible activity; inconsistent; no feedback loop. Compliance theater. |
| **2** | **Acknowledging** | The gap between espoused and enacted is visible and being measured. |
| **3** | **Aligning** | Self-report and evidence beginning to converge; behavioral change is intentional. |
| **4** | **Embedding** | Consistent enacted behavior evidenced across the pillar. Dynamic governance. |
| **5** | **Adapting** | Behavioral patterns are self-correcting; the organization learns from its own enacted gaps. |

Level 0 deserves specific attention. It is not "we are bad at AI." It is "we have no enacted behavior to assess." Activity exists — individuals experimenting, shadow AI running quietly, a pilot somewhere — but no behavioral pattern has emerged at the organizational level. In practice, Level 0 is well-populated: many organizations that believe themselves to be at Level 2 or 3 on organizational maturity are at Level 0 on enacted behavior for most pillars. This is the diagnostic shock the model is designed to deliver.

Level 5 is aspirational by design. Few organizations will reach Level 5 on all seven pillars simultaneously. The value of an aspirational ceiling is that it names what genuine adaptive capability looks like: not a static achievement but a self-correcting system that learns from its own gaps. The word "Adapting" in Level 5 is not accidental — it connects directly to the Adaptive Adoption framework and names the condition every organization is trying to reach in an environment where AI capabilities are changing faster than any fixed maturity endpoint can capture.

**The Level 2/3 boundary** is structurally important and consistent across all seven pillars. Level 2 in every pillar is defined by the same move: the organization becomes aware of its own gap and starts measuring it. This transition — from unaware performance to conscious acknowledgment — is the precondition for any genuine behavioral change. Organizations that are stuck between Level 1 and Level 2 are organizations that have not yet looked honestly at the distance between what they say and what they do.

## 6. Competitive Positioning: What This Model Does That Others Do Not

Twelve major AI maturity models were reviewed in developing the AAMM. Every one of them has genuine value. None of them measure enacted behavior.

The landscape divides broadly into three categories:

**Technical readiness models** (IBM, MITRE) measure infrastructure, data quality, and model deployment capability. They are appropriate for technology teams assessing platform readiness. Humans do not appear in them.

**Strategic readiness models** (Gartner, McKinsey, AIMAA) measure whether organizations have the strategic intent, governance structures, and resource commitments to pursue AI. They are appropriate for leadership teams assessing organizational positioning. They measure what leaders say about AI, not what workers do with it.

**Human-inflected models** (Accenture Responsible AI, EY, Deloitte, Forrester) are the closest competitors to the AAMM and deserve specific acknowledgment. Accenture's model explicitly separates organizational maturity from operational maturity and identifies the gap between them — it is the source of the 72/6 finding that frames this paper. EY's model uses behavioral archetypes and measures the "emotional journey" of adoption. Deloitte frames AI through Human Capital implications.

What none of them have is a three-layer assessment methodology derived from implementation science and behavioral safety auditing. They identify that a gap exists between espoused and enacted. They do not provide the instrument to measure inside the gap. Self-report surveys that ask about behavior are still self-report. Evidence-based audits that check for artifacts are still checking for things that can be produced without changing behavior. The behavioral observation layer — leading indicators derived from what assessors can witness — is what is missing, and what the AAMM provides.

The Filipovic et al. healthcare AI maturity model research is worth specific citation here: their systematic review found that 86% of AI maturity models weight Technology and Data as primary dimensions while under-measuring People and Organization. This is the field's own diagnosis of its own problem. The AAMM is designed as the solution.

## 7. Why This Model, Why Now

The case for a behavior-first AI maturity model is not merely academic. It is practical and urgent.

The AI adoption problem is not primarily a capability problem. Organizations that have invested heavily in AI training, tool procurement, and governance structures are still struggling to close the gap between investment and return. The Super Productivity Paradox is real: individual AI productivity gains are measurable, but organizational throughput has not improved at the rate the tools would theoretically permit. The bottleneck is human behavior, not technological capability.

The trust problem is live and worsening. Kim, Kim, and Lee's 2025 finding — that AI adoption can lower psychological safety and trigger depressive symptoms — is not an argument against AI adoption. It is an argument for doing it in a way that builds rather than erodes relational trust. Organizations that are not measuring trust are not managing it.

The compliance theater problem is expensive. Organizations are investing in maturity model compliance — producing the artifacts that assessments require — without the behavioral change those assessments are supposed to indicate. This is not organizational dishonesty; it is a measurement problem. When models measure inputs and artifacts, organizations optimize for inputs and artifacts.

The People-First opportunity is underexploited. Every resistance narrative in AI adoption — jobs, surveillance, loss of autonomy, skill devaluation — is addressed by a genuine commitment to designing AI for human flourishing. Organizations that make this commitment do not just reduce resistance; they create the conditions for the People-First Flywheel to operate, driving faster and more durable adoption than any mandate-based program can achieve.

## 8. Limitations and Future Directions

This is a v0.1 working paper. Several important limitations should be acknowledged.

**Validation is in progress.** The behavioral indicators and level anchors presented here derive from the research base but have not yet been validated against assessment data from real organizations. The People-First Flywheel hypothesis is theoretically grounded but empirically unconfirmed. The model will be revised as assessment data accumulates.

**Industry variation is not yet modeled.** The AAMM as presented is a general framework. Healthcare, financial services, legal, and government contexts have distinct regulatory constraints, workforce compositions, and trust dynamics that likely require pillar-level adaptations. Industry modules are in development.

**The leadership/governance meta-layer is not yet fully integrated.** An enabling meta-layer — the leadership and governance conditions that make pillar-level work possible — sits above the seven pillars and is not yet fully specified in this version.

**The diagnostic instrument is in development.** The full three-layer assessment instrument — survey questions, evidence requirements, and behavioral observation protocols for each pillar at each level — is being built and will be published in subsequent releases.

**Future research directions** include: empirical testing of the People-First Flywheel interaction effect across organizations; validation of behavioral indicators against adoption outcomes; cross-industry pillar weighting studies; and longitudinal assessment of level progression over time.

## 9. Conclusion

The question every organization is trying to answer is not "do we have an AI strategy?" It is "are our people adopting AI in ways that create value, protect wellbeing, and build durable capability?"

No existing maturity model is designed to answer that question. They are designed to answer a different question: "have we established the organizational conditions that are conventionally associated with AI readiness?" This is a useful question. It is not the same question.

The Adaptive Adoption Maturity Model is the first instrument designed to measure enacted behavior — what people do — rather than espoused capability. It does this by combining self-report with evidence requirements and behavioral observation, drawing on implementation science fidelity measurement, safety culture behavioral auditing, and organizational behavior research that the AI field has largely ignored.

Its central claim is simple: if you want to know whether your organization is genuinely adopting AI, stop asking whether you have the right structures and start watching what your people do. The gap between those two answers is where your adoption problem lives — and where your greatest opportunity for improvement is waiting.
