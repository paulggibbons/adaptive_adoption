\## Strategic Decisions Made



\### 1. Open Source the Framework

\*\*Rationale\*\*: Open-sourcing isn't giving away the product — it's the most aggressive marketing strategy possible. The framework becomes the \*standard\*, and Paul becomes the person organizations call for implementation.



\- RedHat model: framework is free, implementation is paid

\- Community contributions improve and battle-test the framework across industries

\- Signals confidence and native AI fluency competitors can't match

\- Kills the Prosci model (proprietary, gated, $4,500/seat certifications) by making the alternative free, living, and better



\*\*The Prosci Kill Shot\*\*: Prosci needs change management to be static because their revenue depends on controlling it. Adaptive Adoption is the opposite — open, evolving, forkable. You can't compete with free, and you can't compete with a framework that evolves faster than a certification cycle can update.



\### 2. Agent-Managed Maintenance

\*\*What agents can handle now (~70-80%)\*\*: scheduled checks, documentation updates, formatting, CI/CD, dependency management, research monitoring, benchmark updates, PR drafting.



\*\*What still requires Paul (~2-4 hrs/month)\*\*: substantive intellectual decisions about framework revisions when new research contradicts or extends pillar assumptions. Agent flags; Paul decides.



\*\*Meta-positioning opportunity\*\*: "A framework for AI adoption that uses AI agents to maintain itself" — recursive demonstration of what the framework teaches.



\### 3. The Fork Strategy

Industry practitioners fork the repo and contribute back:

\- \*\*Healthcare\*\*: HIPAA compliance modules, clinical workflow integration, patient safety gates

\- \*\*Manufacturing\*\*: OT/IT convergence patterns, safety-critical systems, union negotiation workflows

\- \*\*Government\*\*: Procurement cycle integration, FedRAMP compliance, congressional reporting

\- \*\*Financial services\*\*: Algorithmic discrimination audits, fiduciary duty frameworks



Every fork uses Adaptive Adoption's language and constructs. Every practitioner learns Paul's way of thinking. Community builds what no single consultant could.

\## The Seven Pillars — Evaluation \& Refinements



\### The Pillars as Stated

1\. \*\*Master the Craft\*\* — Build capability through doing, not curriculum

2\. \*\*Embrace Complexity\*\* — Design for nonlinear systems, not waterfall plans

3\. \*\*Consciously Manage Trust\*\* — Address both undertrust and overtrust

4\. \*\*Put People First™\*\* — Augment before automate; ethical stance + strategic sequence

5\. \*\*Design and Prototype\*\* — Sprints and experimentation replace fixed future states

6\. \*\*Prioritize Behavior\*\* — Intention-action gap, behavioral science over attitudes/values

7\. \*\*Manage Ethics Always\*\* — Frontline applied ethics, not governance dashboards



\### Key Refinements Discussed



\#### Pillars 1 \& 5: Not Overlapping (Initial Challenge Withdrawn)

Initial pushback: pillars 1 and 5 share epistemology (emergent vs. planned). 



\*\*Paul's correction\*\*: They address different organizational functions entirely.

\- \*\*Pillar 1\*\* attacks how organizations build \*human capability\*. L\&D's model (predict skills → design curriculum → deliver training) fails when the skill landscape shifts quarterly. WalkMe is the embodiment of the old model — training wheels on a predetermined path. Pillar 1 says the path doesn't exist yet.

\- \*\*Pillar 5\*\* attacks how organizations design \*the change initiative itself\*. Traditional change assumes a known future state. Pillar 5 says you don't know the future state, so prototype and iterate.



Same philosophical root (complexity theory, emergence), different failure modes, different organizational owners. Distinction is valid and important.



\#### Pillar 6: The ADKAR Dismantling

"Awareness, desire, and knowledge do not add up to action." This is a direct, evidence-based dismantling of Prosci's core logic using the intention-action gap from behavioral science. This is one of the framework's most powerful and defensible claims.



\#### Pillar 3: Most Original, Needs Deepening

The undertrust/overtrust duality is genuinely novel — no other change framework treats both as failure modes. Rich academic literature to draw on: Mayer, Davis \& Schoorman's integrative model; Edmondson on psychological safety; ABI framework (ability, benevolence, integrity). Paul should own this pillar harder than any other.



\#### Pillar 7: Major Reframe — Ethics as Practiced Capability, Not Compliance



\*\*The problem with business ethics in practice\*\*: It's a legal risk mitigation function. Governance dashboards tell you whether you \*followed the process\*. They cannot tell you whether the process was worth following.



\*\*Evidence\*\*: VW's defeat device passed through compliance. Enron had a printed ethics code. Wells Fargo had governance dashboards during the fake accounts scandal. Boeing had safety compliance while the 737 MAX killed people. Every catastrophic ethics failure happened inside organizations with compliance frameworks.



\*\*The reframe\*\*: Ethics must be a practiced frontline capability, not a governance function. Same logic as pillar 1 (learning can't be curriculum from L\&D) → pillar 7 says ethics can't be a framework from legal. It must be in the hands of people doing the work.



\*\*Three operational components\*\*:

1\. \*\*Ethical reasoning as a skill\*\* — teachable through case-based reasoning, moral imagination exercises, harm-focused pre-mortems

2\. \*\*Psychological safety as ethics infrastructure\*\* — the VW engineers knew, the Boeing engineers knew, they didn't speak up. Connects directly to pillar 3 (trust)

3\. \*\*Ethics embedded in the sprint\*\* — every sprint includes ethical reflection, not as checkbox but as structured conversation

\## The Lineage Piece



A strategic piece articulating the intellectual traditions Adaptive Adoption synthesizes, and the gaps in each that the synthesis fills:



| Tradition | Contribution | Gap Adaptive Adoption Fills |

|---|---|---|

| \*\*OD\*\* | Humanistic values | Never operationalized for technology adoption; too slow, too process-heavy |

| \*\*Design Thinking\*\* | Prototyping \& iteration | Silent on trust, power dynamics, emotional reality of job fear |

| \*\*Learning Organizations\*\* (Senge) | Systems thinking, generative learning | Never solved the speed problem; can't afford years of learning for AI |

| \*\*Complexity Theory\*\* | Right mental model (emergence, nonlinearity) | Notoriously hard to operationalize; stops at "embrace uncertainty" without Monday-morning actions |

| \*\*Business Ethics\*\* | Principles \& governance | Struggles with frontline operationalization; lives in legal/compliance, not in practitioner hands |



\*\*The claim\*\*: Each tradition contributes something essential, each has a critical gap, and no one has previously woven them into a single operational methodology for technology adoption. That's defensible because it's true.
