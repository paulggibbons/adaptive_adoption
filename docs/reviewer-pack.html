Adaptive Adoption
Change Leadership for the AI Age

Advanced Practitioner Review

What follows is a guide to giving feedback on Adaptive Adoption for change experts. The entire framework is being open-sourced — it is forkable (you can modify from the main branch for, say, healthcare adoption or public sector change) and modifiable (through pull requests — more on how to do that another day). This isn't (yet) interactive, though it soon will be. Written feedback to paul@paulgibbonsadvisory.com — I would welcome the chance to discuss over a call.

It covers four things:

  1. Persistent challenges the field has faced — problems that predate AI but that AI has made urgent
  2. An emerging consensus among advanced practitioners on what has to change in change
  3. Adaptive Adoption — a framework designed to address those challenges, built on three interlocking systems
  4. A comparison table — orthodox change management versus Adaptive Adoption, dimension by dimension

This is not an argument against change management. It is an argument for expanding its jurisdiction. What follows is not ingratitude — it is an honest accounting of what changed while the models didn't.

1. Persistent challenges the field has faced

The Challenge The Underlying Problem What's Needed
Justifying value to executives Since the birth of the field, change practitioners have fought to persuade executives that the people side of change is more complex than comms and training that a 23-year-old with a laptop could do. Execs know AI adoption isn't going smoothly, and "know" that building people capability is critical, but haven't been shown an alternative that looks fit for AI adoption.
No seat at the AI table Often the most important decisions on change are made behind closed doors, by PM teams, dev teams, execs — partly because change has had little to say about harder-edged topics such as governance, and sparse guides for leaders operating in the new era. By focusing on human behaviour change, not policy document development — and by focusing on "governance as enablement" — the change expert's value becomes relevant again.
Professional relevance in the AI era AI is rewriting the rules of knowledge work. Practitioners trained in episodic, planned change have no vocabulary for continuous, emergent, trust-dependent transformation. A framework built for this problem class — one that positions change practitioners as essential to AI adoption, not ornamental.
Closed-source frameworks Prosci, Kotter, and others are proprietary. You can't adapt them, combine elements, or publish derivative work. You rent the framework; you don't own your practice. Open-source methodology that practitioners can adapt, extend, and build on. Value in application and authority, not in hiding the IP.
Commoditised positioning If your competitors hold the same certification and use the same model, where is your distinctive value? ADKAR is a commodity. A practice grounded in behavioural science, complexity theory, and trust architecture that most practitioners haven't touched. Intellectual edge through depth.
No community, no evolution Orthodox frameworks land as finished products: use it or leave it. No mechanism for practitioners to contribute, pressure-test, or evolve the thinking. Frameworks designed to evolve in public — practitioner experience improves the methodology, not just consumes it.

2. What advanced practitioners agree has to change in change

The standard critique of orthodox change management — that change isn't linear, that "resistance" is a patronising frame, that comms plans don't drive behaviour — has circulated for a decade. People nod along and then sign up for their next certification. The critique has failed because it offers diagnosis without prescription.

Among advanced practitioners, a more substantive consensus is emerging. It is not about what's wrong with the old models. It is about what the new requirements actually are:

Emerging Requirement The Shift
Behavioural science integration Fifty years of research on cognitive bias, loss aversion, defaults, and friction — from Kahneman, Thaler, Sunstein, Gigerenzer — has been ignored by every major change framework. A discipline dedicated to changing behaviour cannot continue to operate on a falsified model of rational actors, nor a "spray and pray" approach to behaviour change.
Continuous, not episodic Change has always been continuous, not episodic — but most change frameworks have a beginning, middle, and an end. Inadvertently, in a rush to simplify change, we've trained executive buyers to think of change as a finite, sequential process.
Psychological safety Agreed importance, but practitioners are rarely given permission to intervene where it matters most — leader behaviour. Why? Because if psychological safety isn't front and centre when discussing your approach, you won't get permission. And even when you do, behavioural tools for working with psychological safety are rarely specified.
"Resistance" Advanced practitioners use the word apologetically, knowing that it is a misdescription of a complex phenomenon. Moreover, dealing with resistance is often framed as "tell them more stuff" — the information deficit hypothesis.
Leadership specified behaviourally "Leaders should support the change" is not a specification. AI-era leadership requires named dimensions with observable behaviours, developmental progressions, and coaching frameworks — not generic sponsorship models.
Trust as architecture Trust is not a feeling to be managed through town halls. It is a structural condition with multiple independent dimensions — relational, institutional, self-efficacy, task — each with distinct failure modes. Treating it as a single variable guarantees the wrong intervention.
Ethics embedded, not appended AI raises ethical questions at the speed of a prompt, not at the cadence of a quarterly review board. Ethics must be a frontline practice — practical wisdom in the Aristotelian sense — not a compliance checkbox.
Frictions removed, not installed Most governance frameworks add friction: approval gates, review committees, mandatory training. When governance is slower than bypass, shadow AI forms. The design question is not "how do we control this?" but "how do we make the right thing the easy thing?"
Capability building, not training delivery Measuring "X% of employees using Copilot" measures utilisation, not capability. The goal is a workforce whose capability is expanding faster than the rate of disruption — the rate of change, not the current stock.

Adaptive Adoption is one framework designed to meet these requirements. It has three interlocking systems — twenty dimensions total — each addressing specific challenges from the table above.

3. Adaptive Adoption — how it addresses these challenges

Three interlocking systems. Each page below: what problems it solves, and where to explore further.

Change Agility

The Innovation Flywheel — seven pillars for continuous adaptation

Why am I reading this?

The problems it solves What this system provides
Many change frameworks assume a known end-state. AI adoption doesn't have one. A seven-pillar operating system designed for emergent, continuous change — not bounded projects.
Many change approaches default to training and comms plans. Executives want behavioural outcomes. Capability / Motivation / Trust / Opportunity diagnostic forces behavioural root-cause analysis before intervention design.
Many change toolkits handle complicated change well but break down with complexity and emergence. Built on Cynefin and complexity theory: probe, sense, respond. Safe-to-fail experimentation is structural, not aspirational.
Few frameworks offer a structured approach to trust, ethics, or friction — the issues that actually block AI adoption. Dedicated pillars for trust (Consciously Manage Trust), ethics (Manage Ethics Always), friction removal (Design and Prototype), and people-first capability building.

The dimensions

  • P1: Master the Craft — Skill-based, craft-oriented learning. Contact knowledge, not briefings.
  • P2: Embrace Complexity — Cynefin-informed. Probe, sense, respond. Distinguish complicated from complex.
  • P3: Consciously Manage Trust — RIST Framework: Relational, Institutional, Self, Task trust diagnosed independently.
  • P4: Put People First — Augmentation before automation. Build capability at the rate of disruption.
  • P5: Design and Prototype — Safe-to-fail experimentation. Make the right thing the easy thing.
  • P6: Prioritize Behavior — 20+ behavioural interventions. Observable conduct, not self-reported attitudes.
  • P7: Manage Ethics Always — Frontline ethical practice. Practical wisdom at the speed of a prompt.

The AI Leadership Delta

The Torque — seven behavioural dimensions that turn strategy into observable conduct

Why am I reading this?

The problems it solves What this system provides
Leadership development programmes talk about "supporting change" without specifying what leaders should actually do differently. Seven named dimensions, each with observable behaviours, developmental progressions, and a memorable archetype.
Executives sponsor AI initiatives but don't model AI use themselves. The gap between espoused and enacted is visible to everyone. Active Modeling (The Carpenter) makes visible, honest AI use a leadership standard — including showing the messy drafts and failed prompts.
There is no leadership framework designed for AI adoption — the trust, friction, and ethical dimensions are unaddressed. Dedicated dimensions for trust calibration, friction courage, ethical stewardship, and creative cultivation — the behaviours that differentiate AI-era leaders.
You can't coach what you can't name. Most AI leadership advice is generic ("be a champion") rather than behaviourally specific. Each dimension has a Lead Self / Lead Others / Lead System structure with specific practices at each level. Coachable, assessable, developable.

The dimensions

  • D1: Strategic Imagination (The Prophet) — Protected thinking time. Frontier inputs. The question: what does this make possible that we haven't imagined?
  • D2: Active Modeling (The Carpenter) — Visible building with AI. Public learning. Earned opinion standard.
  • D3: Creative Cultivation (The Gardener) — Ekvall-informed innovation climate. Psychological safety architecture. Immersion infrastructure.
  • D4: Friction Courage (The Liberator) — Dismantle barriers. Every friction installed is a tax on adoption.
  • D5: Trust Calibration (The Empath) — RIST diagnostics applied. Set floor and ceiling on trust simultaneously.
  • D6: Ethical Stewardship (The Helmsman) — Frontline ethical practice. Navigate novel dilemmas at the speed of deployment.
  • D7: Risk Intelligence (The Cartographer) — Map uncertainty across strategic, operational, and reputational dimensions.

Behavioral Governance

The Channel — governance that enables adoption rather than preventing it

Why am I reading this?

The problems it solves What this system provides
Your AI governance framework's primary verb is "prevent." You've optimised for stasis, not adoption. Governance reframed as behavioural enablement. The question: how do we make the right thing the easy thing?
Governance lives in policy documents that nobody reads. The gap between espoused and enacted governance is enormous. Six dimensions that operate at the frictions layer — where governance actually shapes conduct, not where it sits in a PDF.
You measure compliance (training completion, policy sign-off) but have no visibility into actual decision-making behaviour. Three evidence layers (individual, team, system) and Five Dials at board level: Utilization Depth, Capability Expansion Rate, Trust Stability, Iteration Velocity, Leadership Delta.
Shadow AI is forming because the formal system makes doing the right thing harder than doing the unofficial thing. Friction analysis built into every governance decision. If governance installs more frictions than it removes, the design has failed.

The dimensions

  • G1: Decision Rights — Clear, distributed authority for AI decisions. Enabler: who can act, and how fast.
  • G2: Strategic Coherence — Executive ownership of AI outcomes, not delegation to "the AI team."
  • G3: Governance Intelligence — The nervous system. Detect friction, drift, and decision failure before crisis.
  • G4: Risk Intelligence — Dynamic risk sensing, not static risk registers. Integrated ethical reasoning as embedded practice.
  • G5: First-Derivative Talent — Measure the rate of capability change, not the current stock. Accelerated Workforce™.
  • G6: Agent Authority — Governance for autonomous AI systems. Decision boundaries for non-human agents.

4. When to Use Which

Orthodox change management still works for bounded, planned projects with known end-states. Adaptive Adoption is built for everything else.

Dimension Orthodox Change Management Adaptive Adoption
Change typePlanned, episodicEmergent, continuous
End-stateKnown and definedEvolving and discovered
Primary challengeCoordination and communicationBehaviour and capability
Leadership modelSponsorship and championsDevelopmental (Leadership Delta)
Governance modelPolicy compliance and oversightBehavioural enablement
Trust approachAssumed or addressed through commsDiagnosed across four dimensions (RIST)
Behavioural scienceAbsentCentral to methodology
EthicsOut of scopeFrontline practice
FrictionsInstalled for controlRemoved for enablement
MeasurementPlans, training completion, self-reportObservable behaviour across three evidence layers
Capability modelTraining deliveryAccelerated Workforce (1st-Derivative Talent)
TimelineProject-bound with stabilisationOngoing, recursive
AdaptabilityPlan, execute, embedProbe, sense, respond — continuous adaptation
Orthodox change management is a project. Adaptive Adoption is an operating system.
