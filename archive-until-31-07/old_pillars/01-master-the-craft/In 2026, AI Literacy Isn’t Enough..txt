Where the v1.0 Framework Failed
Before we look at the new framework, we have to understand why the old one, and its clones and competitors, broke down, 
Here is what made one of the best literacy frameworks obsolete.
It Was Fundamentally Defensive: The original framework taught risk mitigation and informed consumption. That matters, but it doesn't prepare you for a world where AI is an active agent.
It Underestimated Organizational Paralysis: Teaching failed because organizations treated AI education like compliance training—a checkbox exercise divorced from actual strategy. Skilled employees clicked through modules and went back to their old habits because their organizations hadn't actually restructured around AI's capabilities.
It Ignored Individual Agency at the Frontier: The people building actual businesses with AI weren't waiting for their organizations to certify them as "AI literate." They were learning by doing, failing, and iterating.
It Missed the Economics of Speed and Scale: What happens when AI systems operate at machine speed, across thousands of transactions, with minimal human oversight? Traditional ethical reasoning and metacognitive pauses become impossibly slow.
To solve this, v2.0 of the framework moves from a list of topics to a Mastery Matrix.
It consists of Six Dimensions (the horizontal breadth of what a craftsman must know) and Four Levels (the vertical depth of their capability).
The Six Dimensions of AI Mastery
1. Civics
The first dimension that was ignored by most 2025 frameworks was civics. We got pushback from businesses - “Why teach AI and society?”  This is why.
AI affects parenting, education, art, therapy, medicine, creators, voting, and equality. To make informed decisions on those subjects in your communities, you need facts. You need to know what research says about AI in education—the outstanding results and the negative ones—if you care about how it is used in your kids’ schools.
You need to know how to have the talk with your kids—not about sex or drugs, but about how they responsibly use AI to become super-learners and not super-stupid. You also need to know a little about AI law. What are the points of contention? What does the EU AI Act say, and why does Silicon Valley hate it?
Without grounding in these topics, you're discussing AI in the dark.
2. Metacognition and Critical Thinking
These skills are underdeveloped across the population but are essential when working with AI. Since AI systems are often faster and more knowledgeable than you in specific domains, you need to maximize your ability to parse logical arguments to partner with them effectively. You need to raise your critical thinking game; your cortex needs to do metacognitive pushups.
This requires understanding edge cases and failure modes, when AI is prone to hallucination, how to spot deepfakes or "slop," and how AI outputs interact with your own cognitive biases (otherwise you get a multiplicative effect of both). As the "price" of knowledge drops to near zero, human judgment has to become vastly superior in the Intelligence Age.
3. Applied AI Ethics
Beyond knowing the theoretical ethical dimensions troubling AI development (copyright, energy consumption, privacy), you need to know how to apply these concepts in your own work. What is "Fair Use" and how does it apply to what you are building? When does using AI as a co-creator turn into dysfunctional cognitive offloading? How do you check the veracity of AI outputs that you publish? The key distinction is applied ethics, not theoretical ethics. It's about doing the work, not just quoting Kant and Mill.
4. AI Concepts and Mechanics
Understanding the conceptual foundations—machine learning, how AI systems work, what generative AI actually does—is required to use it competently. You should know what a world model is, what Recursive Self-Improvement means, and the arguments for whether AGI is here, soon, or distant. 
You should be able to define most of these: ML, DL, SFT, TTC, NLP, CNN, RNN, LSTM, GRU, GANs, VAE, GPT, BERT, Transformer, RL, DQN, PPO, RLHF, MoE, LoRA, RAG, CoT.  You should know what a world model is, what Recursive Self-Improvement is. You should know the arguments for whether AGI is here, soon, or distant.
You don't need a PhD in computer science, but you need enough foundational knowledge to understand the tools you wield.
5. Practical Skills
AI is more like carpentry than calculus.  This is the mechanical application of the tools. It moves from basic prompting and context engineering to full-scale workflow orchestration and autonomous agent deployment. (We will cover exactly how this scales in the "Four Levels" section below.)
6. Creativity and Innovation (The Missing Link)
This newly added sixth dimension addresses how to create with AI, and crucially, how to prevent AI from stymying your own creativity.
Stuff from AIDB on problem finding… mapping technology to real problems… spotting white space. (or blue ocean or whatever)
Research from institutions like Cambridge and UPenn shows that merely implementing generative AI does not automatically enhance creativity. In fact, while AI can improve individual output, it often narrows the diversity of ideas across a team because people become anchored to the AI's initial suggestions. True paired creativity requires humans to drive the earliest stages of problem definition and ideation, using AI later for scaling, editing, or selection. You have to build "mindful friction" into your workflows to ensure you don't lose your creative muscle, much like a pilot losing manual flying skills from autopilot overuse.
Innovation with AI is non-linear. Professional creators don't rely on basic brainstorming; they use constraint-based ideation, rapid prototyping, and continuous product-market fit (PMF) testing. A true AI craftsman understands when to let AI lead (exploration and pattern-finding) and when humans must lead (validation against real human needs).
The Four Levels of Mastery
If the six dimensions are what you need to know, the four levels are how deep your capability goes. The jump between each level isn't about speed; it is about a fundamental shift in capacity, structure, and scale.
Level 0: Atrophy/ Avoidance (The Spectator)
Knowing AI exists but doing nothing. You read the headlines, but don’t use the tools. You are skeptical of the benefits, yet your skepticism is grounded in ignorance. When you use it, it is for cognitive offloading rather than co-creation.
Level 1: Advice & Action (The Assistant)
This is where "literacy" stopped. At Level 1, you identify which routine, repetitive parts of your existing workflow, you can hand off to AI to do familiar tasks slightly faster. You know how to engineer context, write effective prompts, and summarize documents. You are using the AI as a sounding board or a junior co-creator.
Harvard research shows consultants at this level finish tasks 25% faster with 40% higher quality. But that is the ceiling. You are accelerating existing work practices, not transforming them. You are using a Ferrari to drive a slightly faster route to the same destination.
Level 2: Automate & Workflow Redesign (The System)
This level allows you to restructure your work entirely, doing things that were previously impossible or prohibitively expensive. This isn't acceleration; it is expansion.
You go beyond chat interfaces and master domain-specific power tools (NotebookLM, Obsidian, Cursor, Descript, Leonardo, Granola). You engage in "vibe coding," orchestrating multiple tools and chaining outputs to create frictionless workflows. Most importantly, you identify work you've never done before because it was too slow or technically difficult, and you do it now. For me, this meant building automated video generation pipelines and running second-brain systems that actually learn.
When you reach Level 2, you aren't doing your old work better. Your work changes shape. You need systems literacy, a willingness to fail, and comfort with tools that don't have manuals yet. The payoff is a 5x to 10x expansion in capability.
Level 3: Architect & Systemic Transformation (The Leader)
Using AI to restructure an entire operating model, create new revenue streams, or build capabilities that become your competitive advantage. You are no longer using AI as a tool; you are architecting a system where AI is the operating agent.
At this frontier, you design decision boundaries that dictate where AI decides autonomously and where human judgment intervenes. You build multi-agent systems that orchestrate across finance, strategy, and operations. This level demands entirely new skills: auditing and governing thousands of AI decisions at scale, exception handling for when models inevitably fail in unpredicted ways, and continuous model performance monitoring.
The jump from Level 2 to Level 3 is about structure, risk, and scale—not speed, but survivability. If you jump here without intermediate skills, you build fragile, opaque systems that fail in production. But if you master this level, you are competing on fundamentally different business models.
The goal for 2026 isn't to create another literacy program. The real question is whether you can design and govern systems where AI operates at scale with integrity, effectiveness, and accountability.
That is the work of the craftsman with AI Mastery.

Where the v1.0 Framework Failed
1. It Was Fundamentally Defensive
The original framework taught literacy—how to read, understand, and safely use AI. It addressed risk mitigation and informed consumption. That matters, but it doesn't prepare you for a world where AI is an active agent in your value chain.

Individual users—even those highly skilled in AI—paid little attention to theoretical frameworks. They were too busy experimenting, failing, learning, and iterating. The five dimensions functioned as discrete competencies. In reality, they're deeply interconnected. You can't practice AI ethics without understanding AI civics. 5. It Missed the Economics of Speed and Scale
The biggest gap: v1.0 didn't address what happens when AI systems operate at machine speed, across thousands of transactions, with minimal human oversight. Traditional ethical reasoning, careful deliberation, and metacognitive pause—all essential in the framework—become impossibly slow at AI scale.
This creates a new problem: as AI systems handle more decisions and operations, human craftspeople need to understand not just how to use AI, but how to architect systems where humans can meaningfully intervene, audit, and correct at pace with machine operations.

Speed and Scale: How to maintain human judgment, ethical clarity, and accountability when systems operate at machine speed across thousands of simultaneous decisions.

Design Patterns for Human-AI Collaboration: Not theory, but actual patterns that work—when to use AI as oracle, as executor, as advisor, as check. Recognizing that different domains need different patterns.
The Craftsman's Responsibility: Moving beyond "know your tools" to "architect systems where your tools serve your values." This includes strategic decisions about which problems to solve with AI and which to leave to humans, even when AI could technically solve them faster.

DIMENSION 6: CREATIVITY AND INNOVATION
The Missing Link: From Calculus to Carpentry
Core Concept: AI is fundamentally different from calculus—it's not about abstract mathematical precision. It's like carpentry: a craft requiring skill, judgment, and intimate knowledge of your tools. This dimension addresses how to create with AI, not just use it, and crucially, how to prevent AI from stymying your own creativity.
Three Sub-Components:
A. Paired Creativity: How Human-AI Co-Creation Actually Works
Research shows that merely implementing GenAI does not automatically enhance creativity—the effectiveness depends significantly on how well human users understand and interact with it. Human-AI pairs don't spontaneously improve their joint creativity over time, even after 10 rounds of creative tasks. Cambridge Judge Business School
The Problem: While AI improves individual output, it narrows the range of ideas overall. When people start with an AI suggestion, they get anchored to it, leading to outputs that are more similar to each other. Upenn
The Solution Structure:
When humans generate initial ideas and AI supports evaluation or refinement, diversity is preserved. Leaders should structure workflows so that humans drive the earliest creative stages, while AI assists with scaling, editing, or selection. Upenn
Human-AI dyads need targeted support and guidance on idea co-development to achieve augmented learning and sustained creativity. Cambridge Judge Business School
The Cognitive Risk: Building "mindful friction" into workflows is essential—ensuring employees practice core creative thinking before turning to AI, much like pilots who lose manual skills when autopilot is overused. Upenn

B. Elements of the Creative Process (Neuroscience-Based)
Understanding how human creativity actually works informs how to partner with AI effectively.
No single brain region houses creativity. Instead, the process relies on collaboration between various areas and networks. The classic four-stage model of creative thought includes: Preparation (building skills/knowledge, relying on the executive control network and prefrontal cortex), Incubation (mind-wandering, activating the default mode network), the "aha" moment (insight, associated with parietal cortex activity), and illumination. BrainFacts
The Implication for AI Pairing:
AI excels at preparation (research, context, pattern recognition)
Humans need incubation (time away, mind-wandering—where AI can become a liability if overused)
AI can support evaluation and iteration, but humans drive the breakthrough moments

C. Understanding the AI Innovation Process
Innovation with AI requires understanding how innovation actually unfolds—it's not linear.
Design thinking involves five key phases—Empathize, Define, Ideate, Prototype, and Test—each serving as a step towards understanding and solving users' needs creatively and effectively. This is a non-linear, iterative process. 7 Fit Framework
The Design Thinking Framework in AI Context:
Empathize: Understand the real problem (AI can surface data; humans interpret meaning)
Define: Articulate what you're actually solving (critical thinking; AI can confuse this stage)
Ideate: Generate possibilities (this is where structured AI prompting matters)
Prototype: Test assumptions (AI can generate prototypes; humans validate fit)
Test: Learn from failure (humans interpret; AI can surface patterns)

D. Product-Market Fit: The Test of Real Innovation
Innovation that matters achieves product-market fit—the alignment between what you build and what the market needs.
Linear achieved PMF by focusing on a well-defined niche, emphasizing craft and user empowerment (values that resonated with target users), and driving growth through product quality and user experience rather than marketing. From the beginning, they prioritized aesthetics and ease of use as core selling points. Eleken
The AI Dimension: Continuously running PMF as an engine—measuring, learning, and iterating based on real user data to steadily improve product fit. The implication: if your business has strong network effects, the core benefit improves as you grow; if you're a SaaS company, you simply must keep improving the product as your user pool expands. Eleken
Critical Success Factor: Focus on one customer, make them love your product, become obsessed with their success. Convince them to pay a meaningful amount. Don't undervalue your product. Then expand: get 3-10 companies to love and pay for it. Watch retention—if early customers leave, they don't count. Eleken

E. Essential Creativity Tools (Beyond Brainstorming)
First-grade brainstorming is insufficient. Professional creators use:
Design Systems & Constraint-Based Ideation – AI can help generate within constraints; humans set the constraints
Rapid Prototyping Tools – Physical or digital; AI can accelerate iteration
User Research Frameworks – Ethnographic, behavioral analytics, predictive modeling
The Double-Diamond Method – Research phase (diverge → converge), Design phase (diverge → converge)
Iterative Testing Infrastructure – Version control for ideas, A/B testing, feedback loops
Cross-Disciplinary Collaboration Structures – AI works best in heterogeneous teams

Why This Matters for AI Craftspeople
This dimension separates consumers of AI output from creators with AI as a tool. A true AI craftsperson understands:
When to let AI lead (exploration, pattern-finding, rapid iteration)
When humans must lead (problem definition, initial ideation, breakthrough insights, validation against real human needs)
How to structure teams and workflows to prevent convergence and maintain authentic novelty
How to validate that innovation actually resonates in market reality, not just in AI feedback loops
The Craftsman's Edge: Unlike people treating AI as a shortcut, craftspeople use it as a real tool—understanding its grain, its limitations, when it serves your purpose and when it leads you astray. They protect their own creative muscle while leveraging AI's speed and pattern recognition.




What This Means Going Forward
The goal isn't to create another "literacy" program. It's to develop AI craftspeople—people who approach AI with the practical skill, intellectual rigor, and ethical grounding that any craft requires.
