# Philosophical Foundations of Adaptive Adoption

## Why This Document Exists

Most change management frameworks have origin stories. Adaptive Adoption has intellectual roots. Each of the seven pillars traces to a distinct branch of philosophy — not as academic decoration, but because the problems of AI adoption are philosophical problems expressed in organizational contexts.

This document maps the lineage. It also makes a claim about the existing scholarly tradition: the philosophy-OD connection has been articulated, but it has never been operationalized. Adaptive Adoption is the first practitioner framework that takes philosophical foundations seriously *and* builds them into pillars, maturity levels, and diagnostic instruments.

---

## The Integrative Argument

AI adoption fails in ways that no single discipline can fully explain. It fails because people don't learn the right things in the right ways. It fails because organizations misread causal complexity. It fails because trust collapses — between humans, and between humans and AI systems. It fails because people are treated as implementation problems rather than ends in themselves. It fails because organizations design in the abstract rather than learning through action. It fails because knowing does not produce doing. And it fails because ethical commitments remain aspirational rather than practiced.

These are not seven aspects of one problem. They are seven distinct problems, each with its own intellectual tradition, each with its own blind spots. No single tradition addresses all of them. That is why Adaptive Adoption holds seven threads simultaneously — not out of eclecticism, but out of necessity.

## The Map

| Pillar | Philosophical Tradition | Core Question | Key Thinkers |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Master the Craft | Epistemology | How do humans come to *know* things? | Aristotle (*phronesis*, *techne*), Polanyi (tacit knowledge), Dewey (inquiry as action), Argyris & Schön (theory of action) |
| 2. Embrace Complexity | Metaphysics & Causality | What is the nature of reality, and how does cause produce effect? | Aristotle (four causes), Prigogine (dissipative structures), Snowden (Cynefin), Stacey (complex responsive processes), Tsoukas & Chia (organizational becoming) |
| 3. Consciously Manage Trust | Social Epistemology & Political Philosophy | On what basis can one agent rationally rely on another? | O'Neill (trustworthiness vs. trust), Fricker (epistemic injustice — testimonial and hermeneutical), Coady (testimony), social contract theory |
| 4. Put People First | Moral Philosophy | What do we owe to the people affected by our decisions? | Kant (categorical imperative — persons as ends), Maslow & Rogers (humanistic psychology), McGregor (Theory Y), Tsoukas & Shotter (phronesis in leadership) |
| 5. Design and Prototype | Pragmatism | How should we act when we cannot know the outcome in advance? | Peirce (meaning as practical consequence), James (truth as usefulness), Dewey (inquiry through action) |
| 6. Prioritize Behavior | Philosophy of Action & Mind | Why do people fail to do what they know they should? | Aristotle (*akrasia*), Hume (reason as slave of passions), Kahneman (dual-process theory), Thaler & Sunstein (nudge) |
| 7. Manage Ethics Always | Applied Ethics & Virtue Ethics | How do moral principles become moral action? | Aristotle (virtue as habit), MacIntyre (practices and institutional ethics), contemporary applied ethics |

## What Each Tradition Contributes — and Where It Falls Short

**Epistemology** explains how to build knowledge through practice but says nothing about trust, power, or ethics.

**Metaphysics** provides the correct causal model for complex systems but cannot explain the intention-action gap.

**Social epistemology** explains the rational structure of trust but does not address how to build learning systems or behavioral architectures.

**Moral philosophy** establishes what we owe to people but does not address how to operationalize those obligations in fast-moving technological contexts.

**Pragmatism** provides a methodology for acting under uncertainty but is silent on moral constraints.

**Philosophy of action** explains why knowing does not produce doing but cannot, alone, build the organizational structures that close the gap.

**Applied ethics** insists that principles must be practiced but cannot build the trust, learning, and behavioral infrastructure that make practice possible.

Acknowledging these limits is not a weakness of the framework — it is the argument for the framework. Adaptive Adoption integrates across traditions precisely because no single tradition is adequate.

## A Note on Thinker Selection

Thinker selection reflects a deliberate tension: credibility with serious readers requires engagement with difficult thinkers; accessibility for practitioners requires that those thinkers earn their place. A few explanatory notes:

**On Prigogine (Pillar 2):** Ilya Prigogine's work on dissipative structures and far-from-equilibrium thermodynamics is the scientific foundation for complexity theory as applied to organizations. His concept of order emerging through instability — not despite it — is more operationally useful for organizational contexts than Whitehead's process metaphysics, though both make compatible claims. *Order Out of Chaos* (1984, with Stengers) remains the accessible entry point.

**On Tsoukas & Chia (Pillar 2):** Their 2002 paper "On Organizational Becoming" is the most philosophically rigorous account of organizational change in the management literature. Drawing on Bergson and Whitehead, they argue that change is not an interruption of stability — it is the ground condition of organizational life. This reframes Pillar 2 from "how to handle disruption" to "how to operate in permanent flux."

**On Fricker (Pillar 3):** Miranda Fricker's concept of *epistemic injustice* — specifically testimonial injustice (having one's knowledge claim dismissed due to identity) and hermeneutical injustice (lacking the conceptual resources to make sense of one's own experience) — applies directly to AI adoption contexts. Employees who lack AI literacy may suffer hermeneutical injustice: they cannot articulate what the technology is doing to their work, and their unease is dismissed as resistance. Trust cannot be built on this foundation. The connection requires explicit argument in the full text; it is not self-evident.

**On Argyris & Schön (Pillar 1):** Their espoused theory vs. theory-in-use distinction — which underlies the three-layer diagnostic in AAMI — is explicitly rooted in Dewey's theory of inquiry and Lewin's action research. They were doing philosophy of knowledge inside organizational development without consistently naming it as such. Restoring that connection matters.

## The Philosophical DNA of OD — What the Field Forgot

The philosophical roots of organizational development were never absent. They were buried as the field professionalized. Three recoveries matter for Adaptive Adoption:

**Lewin's philosophical foundations.** Kurt Lewin's doctoral work was in philosophy of science at the University of Berlin, under the influence of Ernst Cassirer and Gestalt theory. Field theory — behavior as a function of the person and their environment — is a metaphysical claim, not merely a psychological one. Bernard Burnes's archival work has shown that the unfreeze-change-refreeze model, so often treated as Lewin's legacy, is a post-hoc simplification that strips out the philosophical sophistication of the original. Lewin was doing applied philosophy of science. The field forgot.

**Argyris and Schön's Deweyan roots.** Double-loop learning and the theory of action are not freestanding management concepts. They are applications of Dewey's theory of inquiry — the idea that learning is triggered by a disruption to habitual action, requires active experimentation, and produces revised mental models. Naming this connection recovers the depth that practitioners lose when they encounter these ideas as bullet points in an L&D slide deck.

**The process philosophy tradition.** Tsoukas, Chia, and Shotter have spent two decades arguing that mainstream change management makes a fundamental metaphysical error: it treats organizations as stable structures that occasionally change, when in fact organizations are continuous processes of becoming. This is the philosophical foundation for continuous adaptation models — including Adaptive Adoption — and the refutation of episodic change frameworks like Kotter's.

## Why This Matters Competitively

The philosophical foundations of organizational change have been articulated by scholars including Tsoukas, Chia, Shotter, and Burnes. What has been missing is a practitioner framework that takes those foundations seriously. Adaptive Adoption is that framework.

No competing framework can make this claim:

- **Kotter** has no philosophical foundation. It is observation codified as prescription. The 8-step model has no account of causality, no theory of knowledge, no moral philosophy.

- **ADKAR/Prosci** rests on an implicit philosophy of mind that Aristotle refuted twenty-four centuries ago — the assumption that awareness plus desire plus knowledge reliably produces action. This is the *Meno* thesis. It ignores *akrasia*, dual-process cognition, and the entire post-Humean tradition in philosophy of action.

- **McKinsey 7S** is strategy consulting disguised as systems thinking. It borrows the vocabulary of systems theory without the philosophical commitments.

- **Lewin** had genuine theoretical roots (field theory, Cassirer, Gestalt theory), but the model as transmitted — unfreeze, change, refreeze — was designed for a world of punctuated change, not continuous adaptation. The issue is not Lewin's philosophy; it is what the field did to it.

Adaptive Adoption is the first change management methodology built on explicit philosophical foundations spanning epistemology, metaphysics, moral philosophy, pragmatism, philosophy of action, and applied ethics — and the first to operationalize those foundations into a practitioner framework.

## Open Questions for Beta Review

The following connections are asserted but require fuller argument in the book:

1. **Philosophy of language** — Wittgenstein (meaning as use), Austin (speech acts), and Searle's account of institutional reality may deserve explicit treatment. AI adoption is partly a problem of how organizations interpret AI outputs and negotiate shared meaning. This could strengthen Pillar 1 or warrant its own discussion.

2. **The Fricker bridge** — The connection from epistemic injustice to organizational trust in AI contexts needs a worked example or case to be persuasive for non-specialist readers.

3. **The Lewin rehabilitation** — The critique in the competitive section targets the simplified Lewin. A footnote or sidebar acknowledging the real Lewin — and Burnes's recovery of him — prevents the critique from being unfair.

If you see a philosophical connection missed here, or a thinker whose work strengthens or challenges the lineage, open an issue or PR. The framework is open source. The intellectual tradition it inherits is a conversation, not a monologue.

## Further Reading

### Primary Philosophical Texts

**Aristotle**, *Nicomachean Ethics* — Books II (virtue as habit), VI (*phronesis* and *techne*), and VII (*akrasia*). The foundation for Pillars 1, 4, 6, and 7.

**Dewey, John**, *How We Think* (1910, revised 1933) — The clearest account of inquiry as the engine of learning. Essential for Pillars 1 and 5.

**Hume, David**, *A Treatise of Human Nature*, Book II, Part III — "Reason is, and ought only to be, the slave of the passions." The philosophical origin of behavioral science.

**Kant, Immanuel**, *Groundwork of the Metaphysics of Morals* — The categorical imperative and the humanity formula. Foundation for Pillar 4.

**MacIntyre, Alasdair**, *After Virtue* (1981) — The most important contemporary account of how virtues are embedded in practices and institutions. Essential for Pillar 7.

**Peirce, Charles Sanders**, "How to Make Our Ideas Clear" (1878) — The founding text of pragmatism. Foundation for Pillar 5.

**Polanyi, Michael**, *The Tacit Dimension* (1966) — "We know more than we can tell." Foundation for Pillar 1.

**Prigogine, Ilya and Stengers, Isabelle**, *Order Out of Chaos* (1984) — The scientific basis for complexity theory as applied to organizations. Foundation for Pillar 2.

### Organizational Philosophy and OD Theory

**Argyris, Chris and Schön, Donald**, *Organizational Learning: A Theory of Action Perspective* (1978) — The espoused theory vs. theory-in-use distinction. Foundational for the AAMI diagnostic layers.

**Burnes, Bernard**, "Kurt Lewin and the Planned Approach to Change: A Re-appraisal" (2004, *Journal of Management Studies*) — The essential recovery of Lewin's philosophical roots.

**Fricker, Miranda**, *Epistemic Injustice: Power and the Ethics of Knowing* (2007, Oxford University Press) — Testimonial and hermeneutical injustice. Applies to AI adoption contexts in ways the book will develop.

**O'Neill, Onora**, *A Question of Trust* (2002, Cambridge University Press) — The trustworthiness-vs.-trust distinction. Foundation for Pillar 3.

**Snowden, Dave and Boone, Mary**, "A Leader's Framework for Decision Making" (2007, *Harvard Business Review*) — The Cynefin framework. Accessible entry point for complexity thinking in organizational contexts.

**Stacey, Ralph**, *Complex Responsive Processes in Organizations* (2001, Routledge) — The most rigorous application of complexity science to organizational change.

**Tsoukas, Haridimos and Chia, Robert**, "On Organizational Becoming: Rethinking Organizational Change" (2002, *Organization Science*) — The process philosophy foundation for continuous adaptation models. Required reading for anyone building on Pillar 2.

**Tsoukas, Haridimos**, *Philosophical Organization Theory* (2018, Oxford University Press) — The most sustained contemporary treatment of philosophy in organizational theory.

**Tsoukas, Haridimos and Shotter, John**, "In Search of Phronesis: Leadership and the Art of Judgment" (2014, *Academy of Management Learning & Education*) — Aristotelian phronesis applied to organizational leadership. Directly supports Pillar 4.

### On Behavioral Science and Philosophy of Action

**Kahneman, Daniel**, *Thinking, Fast and Slow* (2011) — Dual-process theory. The most accessible bridge between philosophy of mind and behavioral science.

**Thaler, Richard and Sunstein, Cass**, *Nudge* (2008) — The applied policy framework built on behavioral science foundations.

### Mapping Philosophical Approaches to Change

**Smith, Aaron C.T. and Sutherland, Fiona**, *Philosophies of Organizational Change* (2011, 2nd edition 2020, Edward Elgar) — The only book-length treatment mapping twelve distinct philosophical traditions to organizational change. Adaptive Adoption synthesizes across several of the traditions Smith and Sutherland catalog; this is the most useful scholarly comparison point for academic reviewers.

### Academic Venue

**Journal of Organizational Change Management** (Emerald) — The primary peer-reviewed venue that explicitly welcomes philosophical approaches to organizational change. Essential for tracking scholarly development in this area.
